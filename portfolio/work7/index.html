Entanglement Transitions in Random Circuits

Characterizing entanglement phase transitions in qudit circuits.

Duration : April 2024 - April 2025
Supervisor : Prof. Dr. Frank Pollmann
Institute : Technical University of Munich

Here you can find the complete thesis, as well as a poster summarizing it.

Abstract

Random quantum circuits grant a unique insight into the universal dynamical phenomena of generic chaotic quantum many-body systems (1). Discrete-time evolution, under the action of randomly sampled local unitaries, is unburdened by the microscopic features of Hamiltonian evolution, such as symmetries and conservations laws. The dynamics of such quantum circuits can be characterized by entanglement measures, for instance by bipartite entropies or disjoint mutual information (2).

Bipartite entropies grow linearly under random unitary evolution and generically thermalize upon attaining the bipartite maximum. This maximum thermal entropy scales linearly with the system volume, thereby categorizing random unitary evolution into the ‘volume-law’ regime. Further, inclusion of local projective measurements yields hybrid monitored circuits. While weakly-monitored circuits with sparse measurements display a mere reduction in the maximum thermal entropy, a gradual increase in the measurement density leads to strongly-monitored circuits, which display a thermal entropy scaling proportional to the system’s boundary size. This is categorized as the `area-law' regime. At the critical measurement density, the bipartite entropy scales logarithmically with the system volume. This thermal entanglement scaling transition, from volume-law to area-law regimes, has been extensively studied in spin-1/2 (qubit) systems of various spatial geometries (3).

In this work we characterize the entanglement transitions in 1D random quantum circuits of spins (qudits) with arbitrary local Hilbert-space dimension d. This predominantly numerical work employs the generalized stabilizer formalism (4), taking advantage of the Clifford group which forms a unitary 2-design on the space of unitaries (5). Notably, we find the nature of the entanglement transition, from volume-law to area-law regimes, to be preserved for d>2. However, the critical measurement density increases, ostensibly converging to 1/2 in the limit d → ∞.

Moving forward, we are pursuing two directions. First, we are developing a minimal model capturing the entanglement transition in a monitored qudit circuit in the limit d → ∞. Second, with d>2, we aim to understand the application of distinct projective measurements and topologically characterize their corresponding area-law phases, following Ref. (6).
